# Lessons Learned

## Product Lessons

1. Open-source traction does not automatically mean production readiness. Dify's adoption is strong, but enterprise deployment depends on trust, governance, and measurable quality.
2. Logs are not the same as a quality workflow. Users need to move from observation to diagnosis to improvement to release decision.
3. RAG quality is a product problem, not only an engineering problem. Builders need feedback, retrieval diagnostics, and evals in the same workflow.
4. Cost visibility becomes a product feature once workflows include multiple model calls and tools.
5. The best contribution is often a wedge, not the whole platform vision.

## Open Source Lessons

- Maintainers are more likely to accept small, well-scoped PRs than broad strategy drops.
- A strong RFC should include non-goals and open questions.
- Public issues and discussions can reveal product strategy opportunities when clustered across multiple user pains.
- Contribution sequencing matters: filters and exports can create the foundation for evals without forcing a major architecture decision.

## Portfolio Lessons

- A strong AI PM portfolio artifact should show discovery, prioritization, strategy, execution planning, and maintainer empathy.
- The strongest case study is not "I found a feature." It is "I found a system-level opportunity and decomposed it into shippable contributions."
- Mermaid diagrams make product architecture and contribution sequencing easier to review in GitHub.
